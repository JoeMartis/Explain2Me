📘 What makes a good explanation? Guidance & examples — click to expand

A strong explanation doesn't just tell the learner the answer — it teaches the concept behind it. This tool scores explanations against the criteria below. Learners have a chat companion for follow-ups, so an explanation needn't cover every edge case, but it must give a solid foundation.

  • Explains why, not just what — give the reason the answer is correct, don't just assert it.
  • Provides reasoning or conceptual context — connect the answer to the underlying principle.
  • Is substantive — an explanation should be more than one sentence; a single sentence rarely teaches the concept.
  • Adds something new — doesn't merely restate the question or repeat the correct option.
  • Addresses the wrong options — for multiple-choice items, generally explain why the incorrect answers are incorrect, not just why the right one is right.
  • Names options by content, not position — never say “Option 1”, “the first option”, or “choice B”. Answer options may be shuffled when displayed, so a positional reference can point at the wrong answer.
  • Is complete and clean — not cut off mid-sentence, free of copy-paste artifacts.

Examples — a weak explanation and its stronger rewrite

Why does binary search require a sorted array?

✗ Weak

“Because it needs the array to be sorted.”

Just restates the answer — circular, and never explains the mechanism.

✓ Strong

“Binary search works by repeatedly comparing the target to the middle element and discarding half the range. That only works if the data is ordered — the comparison tells you which half the target must be in. On unsorted data a middle comparison says nothing about where the target lies, so you couldn't safely discard either half.”

Names the mechanism (halving via ordering) and says why order is required.

What is the capital of France?

✗ Weak

“Paris.”

One word — correct, but teaches nothing a learner could build on.

✓ Strong

“Paris is the capital of France. It has been the country's political and cultural centre for centuries and is home to the national government, so it's the seat of power rather than just the largest city.”

More than one sentence, and adds context that distinguishes “capital” from “biggest city”.

Which planet is closest to the Sun? (multiple choice)

✗ Weak

“Option 2 is correct. The first and last options are wrong.”

Positional labels break when options are shuffled, and it never says why the others are wrong.

✓ Strong

“Mercury is closest because it has the smallest orbital radius. Venus is farther out despite being hotter (its thick atmosphere traps heat), and Earth and Mars are farther still — so none of those can be the closest.”

Names each option by content and explains why the distractors fail.
