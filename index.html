📘 What makes an explanation great? The criteria, worked examples, and a suggested workflow

A strong explanation doesn't just tell the learner the answer — it teaches the concept behind it. This tool gives instant feedback against the criteria below, so an explanation can be revised and re-checked until it's strong. Learners have a chat companion for follow-ups, so an explanation needn't cover every edge case, but it must give a solid foundation.

  • Explains why, not just what — give the reason the answer is correct, don't just assert it.
  • Provides reasoning or conceptual context — connect the answer to the underlying principle.
  • Is substantive — an explanation should be more than one sentence; a single sentence rarely teaches the concept.
  • Adds something new — doesn't merely restate the question or repeat the correct option.
  • Addresses the wrong options — for multiple-choice items, generally explain why the incorrect answers are incorrect, not just why the right one is right. In particular, address any misconceptions learners may have — the misunderstanding that makes each wrong option tempting.
  • Names options by content, not position — never say “Option 1”, “the first option”, or “choice B”. Answer options may be shuffled when displayed, so a positional reference can point at the wrong answer.
  • Is complete — not cut off mid-sentence.

Examples — a weak explanation and its stronger rewrite

A hash table offers what average-case lookup time?

Options: O(1)  ·  O(log n)  ·  O(n)

✗ Weak

“O(1) — it's the fastest one.”

Asserts the answer with no reasoning, and never says why the other two options are wrong.

✓ Strong

“O(1) on average, because the hash function maps a key directly to its bucket, so lookup doesn't depend on how many items are stored. O(log n) is the cost of a balanced tree or binary search, and O(n) is a full linear scan — a hash table avoids both by jumping straight to the bucket.”

Explains why the answer is right and rules out each incorrect option by name.

Which city is the capital of France?

Options: Marseille  ·  Paris  ·  Lyon

✗ Weak

“Paris.”

One word — no reasoning, and it ignores the other two options entirely.

✓ Strong

“Paris is the capital because it's the seat of the national government. Marseille and Lyon are large, historic French cities, but being populous or old doesn't make a city a capital — that role belongs to Paris.”

Gives the reason and says why the other cities aren't the answer.

Which planet is closest to the Sun?

Options: Venus  ·  Mercury  ·  Earth  ·  Mars

✗ Weak

“Option 2 is correct. The first and last options are wrong.”

Positional labels break when options are shuffled, and it never says why the others are wrong.

✓ Strong

“Mercury is closest because it has the smallest orbital radius. Venus is farther out despite being hotter (its thick atmosphere traps heat), and Earth and Mars are farther still — so none of those can be the closest.”

Names each option by content and explains why the distractors fail.

Suggested workflow

  1. Paste an item below — question, options, correct answer, and the draft explanation.
  2. Review the feedback: the rubric checklist shows which criteria the explanation hits and misses.
  3. Revise and re-check until it's strong.
  4. Want a comparison point? ✨ Make it better generates an example rewrite to weigh against the draft.

Tip: paste any of the examples above into the checker to see how it scores.
