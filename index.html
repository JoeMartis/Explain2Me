📘 What makes a good explanation? Guidance & examples — click to expand

A strong explanation doesn't just tell the learner the answer — it teaches the concept behind it. This tool scores explanations against the criteria below. Learners have a chat companion for follow-ups, so an explanation needn't cover every edge case, but it must give a solid foundation.

  • Explains why, not just what — give the reason the answer is correct, don't just assert it.
  • Provides reasoning or conceptual context — connect the answer to the underlying principle.
  • Is substantive — an explanation should be more than one sentence; a single sentence rarely teaches the concept.
  • Adds something new — doesn't merely restate the question or repeat the correct option.
  • Addresses the wrong options — for multiple-choice items, generally explain why the incorrect answers are incorrect, not just why the right one is right.
  • Names options by content, not position — never say “Option 1”, “the first option”, or “choice B”. Answer options may be shuffled when displayed, so a positional reference can point at the wrong answer.
  • Is complete and clean — not cut off mid-sentence, free of copy-paste artifacts.

Examples

Weak Just restates the answer

Q: Why does binary search require a sorted array?

“Because it needs the array to be sorted.”

Circular — repeats the question without explaining the mechanism.

Strong Explains the mechanism

Q: Why does binary search require a sorted array?

“Binary search works by repeatedly comparing the target to the middle element and discarding half the range. That only works if the data is ordered — the comparison tells you which half the target must be in. On unsorted data a middle comparison gives no information about where the target lies, so you couldn't safely discard either half.”

Names the concept (halving via ordering) and says why order is required.

Weak Too brief, no grounding

Q: What is the capital of France?

“Paris.”

Correct, but teaches nothing — no context a learner could build on.

Strong Reasoning + context

Q: A hash table offers what average-case lookup time?

“O(1) on average, because the hash function maps a key directly to its bucket, so lookup time doesn't depend on how many items are stored — you jump straight to the location instead of scanning. Heavy collisions can degrade this toward O(n), which is why the hash function and load factor matter.”

Gives the reason, the trade-off, and when it breaks down.

Weak Positional labels + ignores wrong options

Q: Which planet is closest to the Sun?

“Option 2 is correct. The first and last options are wrong.”

Options may be shuffled, so “Option 2” can point at the wrong answer — and it never says why the others are wrong.

Strong Names options by content, addresses distractors

Q: Which planet is closest to the Sun?

“Mercury is closest because it has the smallest orbital radius. Venus is farther out despite being hotter (its thick atmosphere traps heat), and Earth and Mars are farther still — so none of those can be the closest.”

Refers to each option by name and explains why the distractors fail.
